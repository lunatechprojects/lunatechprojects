Extraction of Sodium

Down's Electrolytic process

Sodium is extracted by electrolysis of molten sodium chloride. Sodium chloride and calcium chloride are placed within an iron box which has been lined with brick.
(Calcium chloride is added to lower the melting point of the sodium chloride). The grahite anode is seperated from the circular steel cathode by a steel wire gauze so that the products of electrolysis do not mix. A very high current oof upto 30,000 amp is used to krrp the brine in a molten state allowing the Na+ and Ca- ions to move.
At the cathode
The sodium ions gain an elctron each and the sodium metal is deposited and collected.
Na+ + e- → Na(s)


At the anode

Each chloreide ion loses an electron to combine with another chloride ion to form chlorine gas. Chlorine gas is evolved and collected as a useful byproduct.

2Cl- - 2e- → Cl2(g)

	  

              
          

        

      

      


      
      

        

          
Extraction of Aluminium

          
        


        



Aluminium is extracted from its pure oxide by elctrolysis.

To obtain the pure oxide (alumina), bauxite ( hydrated aluminium oxide, Al2O3,2H2O) is dissolved in sodium hydroxide solution under
pressure, forming sodium aluminate (NaAl(OH)4). The solution is filtered and then seeded with pure aluminium oxide.
This seeding causes the aluminium hydroxide to crystallise in a pre state. Subsequent heating to 1200oC in a rotary kiln produces aluminium oxide. The equations
for the reaction are as foolows.


	Al2O3 + 3H2O(l) +2NaOH(aq) → 2NaAl(OH)4(aq)



	2NaAl(OH)4(aq) → NaOH(aq) + Al(OH)3(s)



	Al(OH)3(s) → 3H2o(g) + Al2O3(s)


The pure oxide is then dissolved in molten cryollite Na3AIF6 and the mixture acts as the electrolyte
in the cell. Aluminium is deposited at the cathode which is the carbon lining of the container. Oygen is evolved at the arbon anodes


Al3+ + 3e- → Al(s)

2O2- - 4e- → O2





       
         
   
        

      


      
      

        

          
Extraction of Zinc

          

        


        

          


	Zinc is a moderately reactive metal. Since zinc occurs naturally as a sulphide or carbonate ( zinc blende), the extraction of the metal is by
a reduction method. The ore is roasted in air to obtain the metallic oxide which is then reduced in a furnace by carbon or coke. The zin metal is distilled as
a vapour and collected in a lead spalsh condenser. A solution of zinc in lead is formed. Outside the condenser the lead is cooled until zinc seperates out.
The purity of zinc obtained in this way is 98.5% - 99%.

Zinc is also purified by electrolysis between lead - silver anodes and aluminium cathodes. The zinc obtained is 99.5% pure.
When the sulphide ore is roasted in air, a valuable byproduct is sulphur dioxide.



          
          

	

       


	
	
      

        

          
Extraction of Iron

          
From Haematite ore

	
        


        

          
          
Haematite is first mixed with coke and limestone and roasted in air in a blast furnace. the typical furnace is lined with fire brick is about 30m
 high and 6m wide.Hot air is passed from the base through air pipes. The oxygen in hot air burns the coke, producing carbon dioxide and heat.
	
C(s) + 02 → CO2(g) + heat 

The carbon dioxide rises in a limited supply of air to meet more coke from top of the furnace. The coke reduces the carbon dioxide to carbon monoxide.

	CO2(g) +C(s) → 2CO(g)

The iron (III) oxide is reduced by carbon monoxide at about 1000oC and the molten iron metal produced collects
in the reservoir below the furnace. It is tapped off into moulds, called 'pigs' whre it cools and hardens inot bars. This is pig iron which is not pure.

	Fe2O3(s) + 3CO(g) → 2Fe(l) + 3CO2(g)

The limestone is necessary in the process to remove the silica impurities. At temperatures above 800oC limestone decomposes
to calcium oxide and carbon dioxide The quicklime fuses with silica to form calcium silicate which forms a layer of slag on top of molten iron and is seperated off.



          
          

	

       



	
      

        

          
Extraction of Copper

          

        


        

          
          

Copper pyrite is crushed to a powder and mixed with water and oil. Air is then blown into the mixture and the heavier impurities sink to the bottom of the container.
The froth on the surface containing the concetrated ore is seperated off.The concetrate is then roasted in air in a furnace, producing copper (I) sulphide, iron (II)
oxide and sulphur dioxide gas

CuFeS2(s) + 4O2(g) → Cu2S(l) + 2FeO(l) + SO2(g)

FeO(l) + SiO2(s) → FeSiO3(s)

The sulphur dioxide gas escapes from top of the furnace. Iron (II) oxide is removed by heating it in the absence of air with silica which is added to it in the furnace;
iron(II) silicate is formed as aslag which floats on top of the the molten copper (I) sulphide.

The copper(I) sulphide is then finaly reduced to a metal by heating it in controlled supply of air. The impure copper produced is called blister copper because of the blistered apppearances on the surface on cooling
caused by escaping gases.

Cu2S(l) + O2(g) → 2Cu(s) +SO2(g)

The ipmure copper is purified by electolysis using copper(II) sulphate solution. The impure copper bars form the anode and pure copper forms the cathode

At the anode

Pure copper from the impure anode goes into solution reducing the size of the anode.

Cu(s) → Cu2+ +2e-

At the cathode

Copper ions accept two electrons each and are deposited as pure copper metal on the copper electrode which increases in size.

Cu2+ + 2e- → Cu(s)
